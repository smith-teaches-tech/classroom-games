01 / THE FIRST GATE

Is it THE object, or A object?

Your object must be one specific thing that exists in the world — with a history you can point to. Not a type of thing. Test it: put the word “a” in front of it.

The tool is a rough guide — the real judgement is yours. If “a ___” names the thing you mean, you've got a category. THE / THIS / MY — never A.

03 / THE MODEL, MARKED UP

What a strong paragraph does

This is the Earthrise commentary you read in Lesson 6, answering Prompt 35. The highlighted phrases are doing the analytical work — click any one to see why it counts as TOK and not argument.

Block 1 — real-world context

The Earthrise photograph — NASA catalogue AS08-14-2383, taken on 24 December 1968 aboard Apollo 8 shows the Earth apparently rising over the Moon's horizon. Anders had been assigned to photograph the lunar surface for geological study; the shot of Earth was unscheduled. Before release, editors rotated it roughly 95° and titled it “Earthrise”; the 1968 press caption described it in the register of a mission record — longitude, coordinates, a “telephoto view.” Within a year the same frame ran on the cover of Stewart Brand's Whole Earth Catalog, recaptioned as a fragile planet in need of protection, and was taken up by the environmental movement.

Block 2 — link to the prompt

Read through the concept of values, the photograph shows that even an apparently neutral record is shaped — at the point of production and the point of reception — by what its makers and viewers care about. The “objectivity” of NASA's caption is itself a value, institutional and technical, quietly producing how the image is read. The environmental reading is not contained in the image; it is supplied by viewers who already valued the planet as fragile. Brand had campaigned for an image of the whole Earth in 1966, two years before one existed, which suggests the values preceded the image rather than flowing from it. The same photograph therefore carried different knowledge for NASA and for the environmental movement, because each brought different values to it. Even a photograph, which appears to record reality directly, becomes knowledge only through the interpretation that values make available.

04 / HOW IT IS GRADED

Assessment — A to E

Adapted from the IB exhibition markband. Your whole paragraph earns one holistic letter; the four dimensions below are where the written feedback lands. A = does, on your object, what the model does on Earthrise. Click a band to read it.

AExcellentthe move is clean

Specific object, real-world context clearly identified. One concept genuinely operates as a lens, and the paragraph justifies why this object earns its place. Link to the prompt is clear and explicit. Evaluates how knowledge is produced, justified, or limited — supported by evidence from the object's context. Reads as a convincing single exhibition commentary.

BGoodanalytic, one element underpowered

All elements present and analytic, with one underpowered: the concept does lens-work but isn't fully load-bearing, or the prompt-link is clear but thin, or the evidence fades toward the end. Specific object, a real move, one thing to tighten.

CSatisfactoryattempted, doesn't hold

The move is attempted and TOK intent is visible, but it doesn't hold. The concept sits on top as a label rather than driving the paragraph; or the prompt is named but the body never engages it; or the TOK link is asserted (“this shows knowledge is subjective”) rather than shown. On the TOK side of the line, wobbling. A borderline-generic object lands here at best.

The line the unit is built to catch — did the TOK move  vs.  argued the topic / described the object
DBasicslides into argument / description

Slides into the failure mode. Argues the object's subject matter, or describes and summarises it, with TOK vocabulary applied on top. Often a generic object — generic can't anchor a real-world context, so justification and prompt-link dissolve into generalities. A prompt may be named; the body is argument or description.

ERudimentaryno TOK move

No TOK move and/or no real object. Topic opinion or description only; generic or absent object; no functioning concept-lens; prompt-link absent or sloganistic.

The four feedback dimensions

  1. Object — specific (THE / MY) with a clearly identified real-world context?
  2. Justification — does the chosen concept genuinely work as a lens on this object; does the object earn its place?
  3. Prompt link — is the link to the IA prompt clear, explicit, and effective?
  4. Evidence & analysis — are points supported by evidence from the object's context, and is this analysis rather than assertion?
