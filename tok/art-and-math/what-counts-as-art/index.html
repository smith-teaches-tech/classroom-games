The two answers everyone reaches for

"A real artist made it." "So ask the maker."

Both reasonable. Both die on the same true fact.

Attribution — what the records actually show

Specimen B — the supermarket box — was designed in 1961 by James Harvey: a working commercial artist who was, by night, a genuine abstract-expressionist painter exhibiting at a New York gallery.

A real artist made it. An abstract expressionist designed the grocery box too. The maker can't be the cut.

The maker's own verdict

Harvey walked into Warhol's 1964 opening, saw his box selling as art, and said: "Oh my god, I designed those." To his death he refused to call either box art — not the grocery one, not Warhol's.

Ask the maker. The maker said no — yet the verdict went the other way. So the maker doesn't decide it either.

"What's one man's box, may be another man's art."

— Harvey's gallery, press release, 1964

Naming the thing you've been pointing at

The difference is conferred, not found.

In 1964 a philosopher named Arthur Danto stood in that gallery and asked your exact question: what distinguishes Warhol's Brillo Box from the Brillo boxes Brillo comes in? His answer wasn't on the box.

Danto — "the artworld"

What makes one a work is "an atmosphere of artistic theory, a knowledge of the history of art." You can't see it — you have to know it. The box enters a conversation the cardboard one never joined.

Dickie — conferred status

A community — galleries, critics, curators, the market, art history — confers a status: "candidate for appreciation." Arthood is a title granted, like "champion" or "citizen." No instrument detects it because it isn't a physical fact.

This fills the Community cell on our grid: who certifies, and what? In the arts, the artworld certifies — not correctness, not goodness, but arthood itself.

Callback — "Who Has the Power?"

The power to decide what counts.

Last year: power doesn't only decide who knows what — it decides what counts as knowledge in the first place. Same move here. The artworld's authority isn't the power to price a banana. It's the power to make something count as art at all — to set the category, not just fill it.

✗ Not the claim we're making

"The art market is a racket — rich people and critics gatekeeping for money." Maybe so — but that's a sociology question about corruption. It isn't what we're asking.

✓ The claim we're making

The artworld holds epistemic authority: the power to constitute the category "art." The millions a Cattelan banana fetched in 2024 measure conferred status — not a scam, and not in the fruit (it was eaten and replaced).

Hold the lineWe study how conferral works, not whether the institution deserves it. Keep the banana on "what confers arthood," never on "is the market corrupt."
The trap the lesson exists to spring

If arthood is "only conferred"… is your kitchen banana art?

Here's the cliff: "conferred, not found" gets heard as "so anything goes — it's all made up." Test it.

Cattelan's banana

Duct-taped to a gallery wall, in an art fair, by an artist with a history, inside a conversation about value and objecthood. The conditions for conferral are met.

Your banana

Taped to your kitchen wall. Same fruit, same tape. No artworld receives it, no art-historical position, no candidacy. The conditions are not met — so no conferral happens.

Conferred isn't arbitrary. It's RULE-GOVERNED WITHOUT BEING MECHANICAL.

No measurable test — and still, not anything can be conferred arthood. A status with conditions is exactly what "champion" or "verdict" is. Same structure as a chain of transmission doesn't mean same validity — but it does mean a structure, not a free-for-all.

When conferral doesn't complete

The verdict can stall.

Tracey Emin, My Bed (1998) · my-bed.jpg

A status is conferred by a community — so what happens when the community splits? Tracey Emin's My Bed (1998): her own unmade bed, the debris of a bad week, shortlisted for the Turner Prize. Critics called it a landmark. Others called it not-art, loudly, in the press.

Decades on it sits in major collections — and the conferral is still argued. Certification here is a social act that can fail to complete, complete slowly, or stay contested.

The limit case — a seed for later

Does the conferred thing need a human maker?

We just killed "the maker decides." But conferral still seems to assume a maker behind the object. Press on it: an image generated by an AI, no person choosing the composition — if a gallery confers arthood on it, what exactly got the status?

If conferral is the whole story

…then intent, consciousness, even humanity may be optional. The artworld could confer arthood on a machine's output as readily as on a urinal.

If something resists

…then conferral has a hidden condition we haven't named — and "who, or what, counts as a maker?" becomes the live question.
