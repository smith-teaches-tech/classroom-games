Chapter XIV An Introduction to Physical Geology · 1942

The Formation of Mountain Systems

Geosynclinal Theory and the Architecture of the Earth's Crust

The great mountain belts of the world — the Appalachians, the Rockies, the Alps, the Himalayas — present one of the most striking problems in physical geology. How is it that such immense bodies of folded and fractured rock come to exist, often at thousands of feet of elevation, when the materials of which they are composed are in many cases marine sediments laid down beneath ancient seas? The answer, now well established by a century of careful field study, lies in the action of geosynclines.

A geosyncline is a vast, slowly subsiding trough in the Earth's crust, often hundreds of miles long and bordered by stable continental platforms. Into such a trough, over the course of tens or hundreds of millions of years, enormous quantities of sediment are carried by rivers and deposited in successive layers. As the trough sinks, the load of sediment compresses the underlying crust, and the trough sinks further. Stratum upon stratum accumulates — sandstones, shales, limestones — until the sedimentary fill may be six or seven miles thick.

Fig. 87. Idealised section of a geosyncline. A. The long phase of subsidence, in which sediments accumulate to great thickness within a slowly sinking trough. B. The phase of mountain-building (orogeny), in which the sedimentary fill is laterally compressed by forces attributed to the slow contraction of a cooling Earth, and the strata are folded and uplifted to form a mountain belt.

At length, the long subsidence comes to an end, and the geosyncline enters a phase of mountain-building, or orogeny. The thick sedimentary fill is compressed laterally — squeezed by forces whose ultimate source remains a subject of scholarly inquiry, though most authorities attribute them to the slow contraction of a cooling Earth. The compressed sediments are folded into vast wave-like structures, faulted, and uplifted to form the mountain ranges we observe today.

This explanation, first articulated in coherent form by the American geologist James Dwight Dana in the 1870s and refined by Schuchert, Kay, and others in the twentieth century, accounts elegantly for the principal facts of mountain geology. The marine fossils found at the summits of the Alps and the Rockies are not anomalous; they are precisely what the geosynclinal theory predicts. The folded character of the strata, the great thickness of sedimentary rock in mountain belts as compared with stable continental interiors, and the orderly succession of orogenic episodes through geological time — all find a natural explanation in this view.

It must be acknowledged that certain peculiarities of distribution remain to be explained. Identical fossil reptiles of Permian age are found in southern Africa and South America. Glacial deposits dating from the same Permian period appear in regions now situated near the equator. Some have proposed, on the strength of such facts, that the continents themselves have wandered across the face of the globe — a notion advanced most forcefully by the German meteorologist Alfred Wegener some thirty years ago. This hypothesis, however, requires us to suppose that the solid continents have plowed through the still more solid floors of the oceans, a mechanical impossibility on any sober reckoning. The fossil distributions are far more reasonably explained by the former existence of land bridges, since submerged, which permitted the migration of fauna between continents that have always occupied their present positions.

Continental drift, it may safely be said, has no place in serious geological discussion. The geosynclinal theory of mountain-building, by contrast, rests on a century of accumulated field evidence and forms the secure foundation of our understanding of the dynamic history of the Earth.

Chapter 11

Plate Tectonics and the Architecture of Mountain Systems

A new view of the Earth, assembled in a single decade.

The Earth's outer shell is not a continuous, immobile crust. It is broken into a small number of rigid plates — perhaps a dozen major ones, together with several smaller ones — which move slowly with respect to one another at rates of a few centimetres per year. The continents are passengers, embedded in these plates and carried along with them.

This view, now amply supported by evidence from the ocean floors, has transformed every branch of the earth sciences in the past decade. Mountains, in the new view, are not the product of long subsidence and lateral squeezing within fixed continental margins. Mountains form where plates meet.

Where two plates converge, one is generally forced beneath the other in a process termed subduction. The descending plate carries oceanic sediments and crustal materials down into the mantle, where they are heated, partly melted, and folded into the mountain belts that form at the margin of the overriding plate. The Andes are forming today by precisely this mechanism: the Nazca plate descends beneath the South American plate, and the resulting compression, volcanism, and uplift produce the mountain chain we observe. Where two continental plates collide — as in the case of the Indian plate driving northward into the Eurasian plate — neither can descend, and the colliding crust is thickened and uplifted to form ranges such as the Himalayas.

Fig. 11.4 Schematic cross-section showing the principal features of plate tectonics. New oceanic crust is created at the mid-ocean ridge and spreads laterally. Where the oceanic plate meets a continental plate, it descends in a subduction zone, and the resulting compression uplifts the continental margin to form mountain ranges. The geosynclinal cross-section of the older theory (cf. fig. 87 of Chapter XIV) is now understood as a feature of the continental margin prior to subduction or collision — a small piece of the larger picture, not its foundation.

The evidence for plate tectonics is now overwhelming, but it should be noted that much of the critical evidence comes from a domain inaccessible to nineteenth- and early twentieth-century geologists: the floor of the ocean. The mid-ocean ridges, mapped in detail only in the 1950s, mark the lines along which new oceanic crust is being created. The pattern of magnetic stripes parallel to these ridges, discovered by Vine and Matthews in 1963, records the periodic reversals of the Earth's magnetic field as new crust forms and spreads outward. The age of the ocean floor increases systematically with distance from the ridges — and nowhere on Earth is the ocean floor older than about 200 million years, a fact inexplicable on any theory of fixed continents and permanent ocean basins.

It is now clear why the older geosynclinal theory, however coherent on its own terms, could not have arrived at the present view from the evidence available to its proponents. The decisive evidence lay beneath two miles of seawater. Geosynclines, where they exist at all, are now understood as features of the continental margins where sediments accumulate prior to subduction or collision — a small piece of the larger picture, not its foundation.

Wegener's hypothesis of continental drift, advanced in 1912 and largely rejected for half a century, is now seen to have been substantially correct in its central claim, though incorrect in its proposed mechanism. The continents do move; they do not, however, plow through the oceanic crust. They are carried by the plates of which they form a part. The fossil distributions and Permian glacial deposits that Wegener cited — and that the geosynclinal theory had explained by hypothetical land bridges — are now understood as direct evidence that the southern continents were once joined in a single landmass, Gondwanaland, which subsequently broke apart.

The earth sciences, in short, have undergone a transformation in the past fifteen years more thorough than any since the establishment of geology as a science. What was orthodoxy in 1955 is now of historical interest only.
