No vehicles in the park

A sign bolted to the gate of a public park. Five words. The committee that wrote it thought it was obvious.

In your group — argue it out Which of these breaks the rule? Decide for each:
  • A bicycle ridden down the main path.
  • An ambulance driving in to reach a collapsed jogger.
  • A child's battery-powered toy car.
  • Skateboards.
  • A tank, donated as a war memorial and bolted to a stone plinth.
You disagreed — but not about everything

Nobody read it to mean "no ice cream."

Your group split on the ambulance and the toy car. Fine — those are defensible disagreements. But no one argued the sign bans ice cream, or hats, or singing. That reading is simply out — not because it's unpopular, but because nothing in the five words allows it.

So the readings diverged, yet they were not lawless. Something ruled the ice-cream reading out and let the ambulance argument in. You reached for something the five words don't say. What was it?

What you reached for

A reading is disciplined when it answers to one of these.

01

The words

What does "vehicle" plainly mean? Is a bicycle one? Grammar and plain sense.

02

The purpose

Why was the rule made — and what did its makers mean to stop? An ambulance saving a life may serve that purpose, not break it.

03

The rest of the text

What do the park's other rules say? A reading is checked against everything around it.

04

Precedent

How were past cases settled? Once the warden lets ambulances in, that decision carries.

05

Who decides

When readers split and stay split — whose ruling settles it? A warden, a court, a committee.

A reading that uses none of these isn't a reading. It's a guess.

Rome · the Mass

It becomes the body.

The bread is the body — literally. Its substance changes; only the appearance of bread remains.

Tool used · 04 + 05Precedent and who decides — the reading the tradition has carried, fixed by the Church's authority to settle it.
Luther

"Is" means is.

The body is truly present in, with, and under the bread. You don't get to soften the plain word.

Tool used · 01The words. "Is" is "is" — the plain sense governs.
Zwingli

"Is" means signifies.

The bread stands for the body; the meal is a memorial. "I am the vine," "I am the door" — no one thinks Jesus is a plant or a plank.

Tool used · 03 (+ 01)The rest of the text. Scripture itself uses "is" to mean "stands for" — so the word can carry it here too.
Calvin

Lifted up to it.

Not in the bread — but no mere reminder either. In the meal the believer is truly raised by the Spirit to feed on Christ.

The claim

The text is clear enough to read without Rome standing at the gate. Scripture reads itself.

The test

So — reading freely, in good faith, did they converge? They fractured. Four readings, then forty.

What happened next

Every fracture immediately built its own gate — confessions, catechisms, the Augsburg and Westminster standards. New texts, new authorised readings, new gatekeepers. (Tool 05 never went away.)

You can decide who holds the gate. You cannot run a knowledge community without one.
Can someone outside the frame judge the reading?
Question one

"To me, it's just bread."
Is that a fifth reading — or a refusal to use the tools at all?

Question two

Could you — from outside — say which of the four is the best reading of the sentence?

An outsider can check the discipline: did this reading use the tools — the words, the rest of the text, the tradition — or none of them? That much anyone can grade. But which disciplined reading is the most faithful is settled inside the community that holds the text. You can tell a reading from a guess from outside. You cannot, from outside, rank the readings.

Where this lands

When a text can be read many defensible ways, what makes one reading better-justified — and can an outsider judge it?
