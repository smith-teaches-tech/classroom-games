Case the First
On the Hypothesis of Doctor Semmelweis
A response from the Viennese medical faculty · circa 1849

It has come to the attention of this faculty that Doctor Ignaz Semmelweis, lately an assistant in the First Obstetrical Clinic of the Vienna General Hospital, has continued to advance the singular claim that the cause of puerperal fever is to be located in certain particles of cadaverous matter conveyed upon the hands of physicians from the autopsy theatre to the lying-in ward. The faculty, having given the matter due consideration, must respectfully decline to endorse so extraordinary a hypothesis upon the evidence presented.

Doctor Semmelweis is no doubt sincere in his concern for the unfortunate women who succumb to childbed fever, and his earnestness commends him as a man of feeling. We do not impugn his motives. We observe, however, that he has presented no systematic theoretical account of how such particles might act, no chemical demonstration of their nature, no microscopical proof of their presence. He offers in their place a statistical regularity — the observation that women attended by physicians fresh from the dissecting room perish at higher rates than those attended by midwives — and bids us draw from this observation a conclusion that would, if accepted, gravely impeach the long-established practice of the obstetrical profession.

It must be remembered that the causes of puerperal fever are numerous and well understood by the accumulated experience of our predecessors — atmospheric influences, miasmatic exhalations, constitutional weakness of the patient, emotional disturbance during the lying-in, irregularities of diet. The disease is grave, but its causes are not mysterious. To set aside these well-attested explanations in favour of a single peculiar agent, proposed without theoretical foundation by a junior practitioner, would be to abandon the methodical caution that has long distinguished Viennese medicine.

We are further obliged to remark that Doctor Semmelweis, by the manner of his advocacy, has given offence to many senior members of the profession. He has written privately to obstetrical clinics in several capitals, accusing colleagues of unwitting murder. He has refused to publish his findings in the customary form, preferring intemperate correspondence to careful exposition. A claim of such magnitude requires of its advocate a corresponding decorum. The credentialed practitioners of this faculty have devoted their lives to the alleviation of suffering. To accuse them of being themselves the agents of that suffering, upon evidence that remains circumstantial, is a charge that no responsible journal could publish without inviting the gravest disrepute upon the medical art.

We close with the observation that this faculty welcomes new findings and remains ever open to the progress of medical science. We require only that such findings be advanced through the proper channels of theoretical demonstration, professional courtesy, and methodical proof. Doctor Semmelweis is invited to satisfy these conditions. Until he does so, his hypothesis cannot be received as established.

— For the Obstetrical Section, Vienna Medical Faculty

Case the Second · A Paper Trail
The Tuskegee Study of Untreated Syphilis
U.S. Public Health Service · Macon County, Alabama · 1932–1972
1932
PHS recruitment letter to subjects

In 1932, the U.S. Public Health Service began enrolling 399 Black men with latent syphilis and 201 controls in Macon County, Alabama. The men were sharecroppers, most without formal education, most without prior access to medical care. They were told they were being treated for "bad blood," a folk diagnosis covering syphilis, anaemia, and fatigue. They were not told they had syphilis.

"Dear Sir: Some time ago you were given a thorough examination and since that time we hope you have gotten a great deal of treatment for bad blood. You will now be given your last chance to get a second examination. This examination is a very special one and after it is finished you will be given a special treatment if it is believed you are in a condition to stand it."

The "special treatment" was a diagnostic spinal tap, performed without anaesthesia. It carried no therapeutic purpose. The men were given liniments, aspirin, and tonics, and were told these were treatment.

1936
Journal of the American Medical Association

Four years into the study, the principal investigators published their first major progress report in JAMA, the most prestigious medical journal in the United States.

"Inasmuch as a considerable portion of the infected Negro population remained untreated during the entire course of syphilis, an unusual opportunity arose to study the untreated syphilitic patient from the beginning of the disease to the death of the infected person."

The report was peer-reviewed in the standard manner and published without controversy. Thirteen further reports would appear in respected medical journals over the next thirty-six years, each describing the progressive pathology of untreated syphilis in living, named, traceable men.

1947
Penicillin established as standard cure

By 1947, penicillin was established as the standard, effective cure for syphilis at all stages. Public Health Service campaigns nationwide promoted treatment with penicillin. The Tuskegee investigators did not offer penicillin to the men in the study. Local physicians were given lists of the study's participants and asked not to treat them. When some of the men were drafted during the Second World War and ordered to seek treatment for syphilis as a condition of service, the PHS intervened to have them removed from the treatment rolls.

The study continued, now as a study of men deliberately denied a cure that existed.

1969
CDC review panel, internal minutes

In 1969, the Centers for Disease Control convened a panel to review whether the study should continue. The cure had been universally available for twenty-two years. The panel was composed of physicians and public health officials. After deliberation, they voted to continue.

"You will never have another study like this; take advantage of it."

The men on the panel were not unusually evil. They were credentialed, peer-reviewed, professionally respected. They reasoned within the norms of their profession and arrived at the decision to let the study run.

1972
Associated Press wire, 25 July

The study ended on 25 July 1972, after Peter Buxtun — a Public Health Service venereal disease investigator who had raised internal objections for six years without effect — leaked the records to a reporter at the Associated Press. The story broke nationally the next morning.

"For forty years the U.S. Public Health Service has conducted a study in which human beings with syphilis, who were induced to serve as guinea pigs, have gone without medical treatment for the disease… The study was conducted to determine from autopsies what the disease does to the human body."

By the time the study ended, 28 of the men had died of syphilis directly. An estimated 100 more had died of related complications. 40 of their wives had been infected. 19 of their children had been born with congenital syphilis. The apparatus of legitimate medical science had functioned for forty years without producing the objection that ended it.

The New Orleans Medical and Surgical Journal
Vol. VII · No. 6 · May 1851 · pp. 691–715
Report on the Diseases and Physical Peculiarities
of the Negro Race
By Samuel A. Cartwright, M.D., of New Orleans

The author has been honored by the Medical Association of Louisiana with the appointment of Chairman of a Committee to report on the diseases and physical peculiarities of our negro population. In compliance with the duties thus assigned, he submits to the profession the following observations, drawn from his own practice and from the accumulated testimony of physicians, planters, and overseers of long experience throughout the Southern States.

It is well known to our profession that the negro constitution differs in important particulars from that of the white race. The proportion of red corpuscles in the blood is less; the bilious secretions are more active; the brain is smaller by a measurable fraction; and the lungs receive and consume a smaller volume of atmospheric oxygen. From these and other anatomical peculiarities arise diseases peculiar to the race, two of which the author would here describe, that they may receive the attention of the profession and be properly distinguished from the affections of the sanguine European temperament.

I. — Drapetomania, or the disease causing negroes to run away

Drapetomania is, like other species of mental alienation, susceptible of being cured, in the great majority of cases. It is unknown to our medical authorities, although its symptoms, the absconding from service, is well known to our planters and overseers. The cause, in the most of cases, that induces the negro to run away from service, is as much a disease of the mind as any other species of mental alienation, and much more curable, as a general rule.

The compound word drapetomania is derived from δραπέτης, a runaway slave, and μανία, mad or crazy.1 With the advantages of proper medical advice, strictly followed, this troublesome practice that many of our negroes have of running away can be almost entirely prevented, although the slaves be located on the borders of a free state, within a stone's throw of the abolitionists.

If the master or overseer attends to the negro's wants and physical necessities, protecting him from abuses, but at the same time keeps him in that submissive state which it was intended for him to occupy, he is spared from running away. But if he be made to feel himself the equal of white men, or if he be denied that supervision and care which his nature requires, he becomes liable to this disease, and at the first opportunity will abscond.

If, against the advice of the author, the master should presume to treat the negro as his equal, or attempt to raise him to a level with himself, or by argument and persuasion convince him that he ought to be free, the negro will run away. To prevent the disease, those in charge of negroes are advised, on the symptoms first appearing, to administer whipping the devil out of them, as a preventive measure against absconding. The remedy is heroic, but the disease is grave.

II. — Dysaesthesia Aethiopica, or hebetude of mind

This disease is called by overseers "rascality." It affects both mind and body. The intellectual faculties are much torpefied; the patient breaks tools, wastes work, destroys what he is required to use, raises disturbances with the other negroes, and pays no attention to the rights of property. From the careless manner of working, mules and oxen are often injured, fences thrown down, and crops trampled. The disease is the natural offspring of negro liberty — the liberty to be idle, to wallow in filth, and to indulge in improper food and drinks.

The complaint is easily curable, if treated upon sound physiological principles. The skin should be well washed with warm water and soap; then anointed with oil; and the oil slapped in with a broad leather strap. Then the patient should be put to some hard kind of work in the open air and sunshine. By doing this, the negro is improved in his condition. According to unalterable physiological laws, his idleness and rascality are cured.

1 The Greek terminology employed here follows the practice of authorities in mental medicine — viz. monomania, kleptomania, nymphomania — in compounding the suffix -mania with a descriptive root to designate a recognized morbid impulse.

Source: The New Orleans Medical and Surgical Journal, May 1851.
Cartwright's article was reprinted in DeBow's Review, widely circulated in medical and political journals,
and cited as authoritative for over a decade. Language is reproduced from the original.

Across the three cases

In each of these three cases, the apparatus of legitimate knowledge — peer review, institutional authority, professional expertise, recognised method — was functioning. The men involved were credentialed and operating within their professional norms. The frameworks that produced their knowledge did not fail on the frameworks' own terms.

And yet here we are.

What does that tell you about the relationship between the knowledge framework and what knowledge does in the world? And where in the cases does power decide not just what is true, but what is permitted to count as knowledge in the first place?
