How big is the overlap?

A biologist, Stephen Jay Gould, proposed NOMA — “non-overlapping magisteria”: science and religion never touch, so they can never conflict. The loudest voices on the other side say the opposite — it’s total war, all the way across. You just built the map. Which is right?

The NOMA claim
Overlap = none
Tidy, but your cards broke it: origins, miracles, prayer-as-effective all sat in the middle. The circles do touch.
The culture-war claim
Overlap = total
Also broken: “boiling point,” “what causes malaria,” “what makes a death meaningful” sat cleanly on one side. Most questions never met.
What the cards show
Overlap = small, and real
Not zero, not everything. A few questions are genuinely contested by both — and that narrow band is where the friction actually lives.
The two grids meet here

Science’s blind spot is religion’s home ground.

The R cell isn’t a weakness invented for religion. We ran it on science first — and the answer slots straight into the gap.

Science · R cell (NS L3)

Cannot reach what a death means, what one ought to do, what makes a life worth living. The method measures; it does not weigh meaning.

Religion · R cell (this lesson)

Cannot reach the empirically checkable — the age of rocks, the cause of a fever. Its method addresses exactly what science marked off-limits.

Same instrument, different answers. Each reaches a kind of truth the other cannot — and each is tempted to overreach into the other’s ground. That temptation has a name and a place: the overlap.

The directed landing / not relativism

There is a fact about which question this is.

“It’s all just opinion” is the easy exit — and it’s wrong. Whether “the boiling point of water” is an empirical question is not a matter of taste or culture; a thermometer settles it and a sermon does not. The boundary is real. So is the overlap — and at the overlap, the conflict is real too.

But notice the cut. TOK can say this card sits in the overlap — origins, a miracle, healing-prayer are claims both methods make about the same fact. TOK does not say who is right there. Whether a religious claim is true is not the kind of question this instrument reaches — that was the rule the whole unit obeyed. And the overlap doesn’t force one answer: traditions themselves differ on how to hold it — reinterpret the text, reject the science, or hold both in tension. The overlap is a place, not a verdict.

Write the paragraph

Name what each method can reach and what it cannot. Use at least one overlap card as evidence that the magisteria touch — and say why “overlap = none” and “overlap = total” are both wrong. Then draw the cut TOK keeps: it can name the scope-collision; it cannot, from here, say who is right inside it.
