Pair 01 · Two World Projections

Mercator and Gall–Peters.
Both are true maps of the same world.

Projection A
Mercator (1569)
Projection B
Gall–Peters (with Tissot's indicatrices)

On Mercator, Greenland looks larger than Africa. Africa is fourteen times larger. Gall–Peters fixes that — at the cost of recognisable shapes. The small circles on Gall–Peters (Tissot's indicatrices) all have the same area — that's what "equal-area" means.

Pair 03 · Two Photographs of the Same Event

One day. Two photographs. The March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom · 28 August 1963.

Photo A · Aerial
The crowd from above
Aerial view of the crowd, c. 200,000–300,000 people · Library of Congress / U.S. News & World Report Collection · Public domain
Photo B · Close-up
A face in the crowd
Edith Lee-Payne, age 12 · photo: Rowland Scherman for USIA · National Archives · Public domain

Both photographs are true. Both were taken that day. Each one shows the protest. They tell completely different stories about what the protest is.

A footnote worth raising in discussion: Edith Lee-Payne did not know she had been photographed until 2008 — 45 years later. The photograph carried knowledge about her that she did not have about herself.

Phase 3 · The Generalisation
The map is not
the territory.
Alfred Korzybski · 1933

A scientific model is a map of a system.
A historical narrative is a map of the past.
A statistic is a map of a population.
A news photograph is a map of an event.

Every map selects. Every map distorts.
That's not a failure. That's what a map is.
