Signs, Convention & Authority

A sign

A symbol like this is designed to need no words — to mean the same thing to everyone on Earth, in any language.

Open the floorDoes it? Is there such a thing as a sign that means the same thing to everyone — or does every sign carry something it doesn't admit?

Person
Men
Women

These cross language borders fast — that part is real. Point at the airport sign in Tokyo or São Paulo and you'll find the toilet. Denotation travels.

Specimen 1 · the turn

The "neutral" human is not neutral.

Debate it first — then reveal.

The figure that stands for "Person" is identical to the one that stands for "Men." The default human wears trousers. "Women" is that same figure with a skirt added — the marked deviation from a male norm.

The sign claims to reach everyone. To do it, it quietly decided who the default person is. Its universality fails — not at translation, but at presupposition.

BARTHES · MYTH — a convention that has come to look natural. The "neutral" figure feels like simple fact, not a choice. That feeling is the myth doing its work.
Push itWhose perspective got built into the "neutral"? Who has to notice they've been left out before anyone else can see it at all?

The tools you just used

You felt these before we named them.

SIGNIFIER — the mark itself (the figure, the colour).  SIGNIFIED — the idea it points to (toilet, "this kind of person").
ARBITRARY — nothing in the mark forces its meaning; the link is fixed by agreement, not by nature.  CONVENTION — that agreement, held by a community.
MYTH (Barthes) — a convention that disguises itself as nature, so the reader stops seeing the choice.

Keep these four. The next specimen runs on them.

Specimen 2 · block the easy answer

"So East and West just think in opposites."

It's the obvious conclusion. Debate whether it holds — then reveal.

Hong Kong — a Chinese context — uses the Western code: green up, red down. So it isn't culture-as-destiny.

If culture doesn't fix the meaning, what does? A community and an authority — which exchange, which trading software, which regulator adopted which convention. The sign is arbitrary (Saussure); someone holds the power to fix it.

CALLBACK · L1 / L2 — this is the same power you tracked in expat vs migrant and in the euphemism releases: authority over what a sign is allowed to mean.

The cut TOK keeps

If the same sign means opposite things and neither is wrong — where does the meaning live?

Part of the meaning is in the sign. Part is in the community reading it. The mark alone never carries it the whole way.

So a reader who doesn't know whose code is running will misread reliably — and, worse, won't know they're misreading. The sign looks self-explanatory the entire time.

Now write

~200 words. One specimen as your evidence.

A sign system can look universal or natural and still carry a presupposition or a convention that decides what it means. Using the pictogram or the colour code:
  1. Name the presupposition or convention in your specimen.
  2. Name who holds authority over it.
  3. State what a reader who didn't know the code would wrongly "know."
  4. Close on the cut: which part of the meaning is in the sign, and which part is in the community reading it?

Where this goes

The thread doesn't stop at language.

→ Maths. Notation is a sign system too. Does it just record thought, or does it let you think things you otherwise couldn't? (Zero. The calculus notation that opened a century of physics.)

→ The arts. Some signs carry what no proposition can — a colour, a chord, a title that renames the image beneath it. Meaning that isn't a statement, and can't be paraphrased without loss.

Same question, new material: what does the sign let us know — and who decides?
