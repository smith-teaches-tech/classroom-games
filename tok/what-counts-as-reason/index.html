Way 1Case 1 of 4
A way of deciding what counts as a legitimate reason to accept a teaching as genuine

A teaching is preserved by recording the unbroken line of people who passed it down, one to the next, from the founding figure to today. Two checks run before a report is accepted. The chain: every person in the line is investigated — honest? reliable? accurate of memory? — and the report is graded only as strong as its weakest carrier; a break, or one unreliable carrier, downgrades it however good it sounds. The content: even a perfectly transmitted report is set aside if it contradicts the community's most firmly established teachings. Certified by: trained specialists in the carriers' lives — not the ordinary reader.

Way 2Case 2 of 4
A way of deciding what counts as a legitimate reason to accept a teaching as genuine

A person may seek personal confirmation of a teaching directly: study it carefully, then ask in prayer, expecting an inward witness that it is true. This counts as real knowledge — but it is bounded by three rules. It is trusted only for the person's own life and responsibilities, not for redefining the teaching for everyone. It must agree with the established written scripture. And a teaching binding on the whole community can come only through the one recognised leader, never from a private individual's experience. An inward witness that breaks these bounds is treated as mistaken or counterfeit, not as authority.

Way 3Case 3 of 4
A way of deciding what counts as a legitimate reason to accept a teaching as genuine

The authority to say what the founding texts mean does not rest with each individual reader; it rests with a recognised teaching office that has carried the interpretation across generations. When a person reports a private vision or a strong inner prompting, it is not accepted automatically — it is submitted to that office and tested against the established texts and inherited tradition, and trained guides apply set rules for telling a genuine prompting from a misleading one. Even a vision the office judges trustworthy adds nothing binding to the core teaching: it is permitted belief, not required. Certified by: the teaching office — not the individual visionary.

Way 4Case 4 of 4
A way of deciding what counts as a legitimate reason to accept a teaching as genuine

When someone claims "this teaching comes from the founder himself," the claim is not accepted on the strength of the source — not even if the source is a famous elder, a whole community of teachers, or a respected senior. The procedure is fixed: lay the claimed teaching beside the established collection of the founder's recorded discourses and the community's rule of conduct. If it fits and agrees with them, accept it as authentic; if it does not, reject it — however senior or sincere the person reporting it. The test is the fit with the canon, not the prestige of the speaker.
