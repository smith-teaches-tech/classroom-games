    Step 2 of 5 · How the mind changes

    Settled — and then not.

    Both pages below were the consensus science of their moment. Read what each treats as obvious.

    SETTLED 1942
    1942
    Geology textbook · geosynclinal theory

    The continents are fixed in place. Mountain ranges rise where the crust buckles and sags vertically over long ages.

    Wegener’s notion of drifting continents is dismissed: no known force could plough a continent through the ocean floor. The matching coastlines of Africa and South America are a coincidence of erosion.

    SETTLED 1968
    1968
    Geology textbook · plate tectonics

    The continents move, carried on plates of the Earth’s surface. Mountains rise where plates collide.

    Wegener was substantially right. The matching coastlines are not coincidence — they are a record of a single landmass that split apart.

    What happened between 1942 and 1968 was not a better argument. New evidence arrived that neither framework could ignore — magnetic stripes mirrored on the seafloor either side of mid-ocean ridges, recording the spreading itself. The community didn’t vote; the old framework had no category that could hold the new data, so the framework had to give.

    Record · Q2
    “The continents are fixed” was settled science in 1942. What forced the change — a more persuasive argument, or something else? What does that tell you about how science changes its mind?
