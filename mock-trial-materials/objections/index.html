How to play  ·  the strategy of objecting

Whole-class game. The trial runs in order: Prosecution's witnesses first (direct, then defense cross), then Defense's witnesses (direct, then prosecution cross). Each round shows the examination so far (faded), then the one line to judge. One team is "on the clock" — the team that would be objecting. Read it aloud, let the team debate, lock in their call, and reveal the ruling.

  • Object — and name the type. A clean, correct objection scores. You must say which objection it is.
  • Watch how a direct exam BUILDS. Lawyers lay foundation step by step with open questions. A question is judged in the context of what came before it — that's why the lead-up is shown.
  • Sometimes DON'T object. A leading question is fine on cross; a witness describing what they saw is fine; "hearsay" is fine if the speaker is testifying. Objecting to these just tells everyone you don't know the rules.
  • Some slips are GOOD to let ride. If a witness blurts out an opinion or hearsay that helps your side, the jury already heard it. Letting it stand can be the smart move.
  • Getting caught leading on Direct is the bad kind. It looks sloppy. Watch the Direct / Cross flag every round — it changes the answer.
  • Argue it. When an objection can be challenged (relevance, expert opinion, speculation), the other team gets to argue the evidence should come in — and can score too. Leading and hearsay cannot be argued: apologize and rephrase or drop.

Both teams have roughly the same points available, so neither side is favored. Use the + / − buttons to adjust any score by hand. Keyboard: ← → to move, R to reveal.
