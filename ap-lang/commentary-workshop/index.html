‚ö† The problem we're fixing
Most students write commentary that moves horizontally ‚Äî piling on new examples, new claims, or unconnected ideas. A scorer reading your paragraph tracks every transition. If your commentary signals "new point," your line of reasoning looks broken ‚Äî even if you were actually going deeper.

Today you'll practice the three moves that keep your paragraph on the rail.
M

Mechanism

Evidence doesn't prove a claim on its own ‚Äî there's always a logical gap between what the evidence shows and what you're arguing. The mechanism closes that gap. It explains why your evidence actually supports your claim. Without it, the reader has to make the connection themselves ‚Äî and they might not make it the way you intend.

"Why does this evidence prove my claim? What is the logical bridge between the two?"

C

Consequence

Once you've explained how something works, you push one level further: what does this lead to? A consequence is a result or outcome that follows logically from your claim and mechanism. Be careful ‚Äî a consequence is a prediction, not a restatement. It happens because of the mechanism. It doesn't just repeat what you already said in different words.

"If this is true, what actually happens as a result?"

I

Implication

This is the deepest move. You've explained the mechanism and the consequence ‚Äî now you zoom out and answer the "so what?" A strong implication connects your argument to something larger: a principle, a pattern, a question about fairness or society or human nature. It's not a new argument ‚Äî it's the full weight of the argument you've been building.

"Why should anyone beyond this situation care about this?"
