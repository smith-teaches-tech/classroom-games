The Barbara Jordan Prompt

Read the full prompt carefully. We'll unpack it together before looking at thesis models.

The source text

Barbara Jordan — U.S. Representative, 1972–1979

"[T]his is the great danger America faces — that we will cease to be one nation and become instead a collection of interest groups: city against suburb, region against region, individual against individual; each seeking to satisfy private wants."

The task

Write an essay that argues your position on Jordan's claim that "private wants" threaten national identity.

In your response you should do the following:

1

Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.

2

Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.

3

Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.

4

Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

Let's unpack it

"private wants"

What does Jordan mean by this? What are some examples — personal ambition, consumer choices, individual priorities? Does the phrase carry a negative connotation, or is it neutral?

"national identity"

What holds a nation together? Shared values, history, language, institutions? Is national identity something fixed, or does it shift over time?

"threaten"

Jordan says private wants are a danger. Is this a causal claim — that private wants directly cause the erosion of national identity? Or a conditional one?

"collection of interest groups"

Jordan paints a specific picture: city vs. suburb, region vs. region. What does fragmentation look like in practice? Is this inevitable, or can it be resisted?

Before you write — three possible stances

Defend

Jordan is right. Private wants do erode national unity. When people prioritize themselves over the common good, the bonds that hold a nation together weaken.

Challenge

Jordan is wrong. Private wants don't threaten national identity — they may even strengthen it, because people invest in a nation that gives them freedom to pursue their own goals.

Qualify

Jordan is partially right. Private wants are only a threat under certain conditions — when they crowd out any sense of shared responsibility, or when institutions fail to connect individual and collective wellbeing.
